## Introduction — Whose Celebration Is the Breakthrough in Life Science LLMs?

On April 16, 2026, OpenAI released GPT-Rosalind. Named after Rosalind Franklin, the discoverer of DNA's double helix structure, this is OpenAI's first frontier reasoning model purpose-built for the life sciences. Reuters, VentureBeat, and Euronews all covered the announcement on the same day. In its official statement, OpenAI claimed GPT-Rosalind can "accelerate drug discovery, genomics analysis, protein reasoning, and scientific workflows."

But what truly deserves attention is something else: GPT-Rosalind was not opened to the public. Its access is tightly restricted to a vetted set of enterprise users, and currently only institutions based in the United States are eligible to apply. OpenAI did not explain when or under what conditions this restriction might be lifted. Biologists around the world can read its technical report, but cannot use it.

In the limited technical report that was publicly released, one passage went largely unnoticed: GPT-Rosalind, without any human guidance, independently completed optimization calculations for the folding pathways of multiple complex proteins, achieving accuracy and efficiency surpassing all previous specialized models. It was trained on over fifty common biological experiment workflows and can directly query public biological databases, read scientific papers, propose new drug target hypotheses, and design experimental protocols. This is not a language model that merely excels at "talking about" biology; it is a general intelligence system capable of directly engaging in core biomedical computing tasks. From drug target screening to personalized gene therapy design, from early diagnosis of rare diseases to optimization of cancer immunotherapies, it can accomplish in hours what a top medical team would need years to complete.

And this capability, at this moment, is held by fewer than ten institutions.

In Rochester, Minnesota, the International Patient Services department of the Mayo Clinic receives appointment requests from around the world every day. The process is highly mature: patients submit medical records, undergo remote pre-screening, and are told the wait time. Those with international patient status — typically holders of certain passports with ample commercial insurance or the ability to pay entirely out of pocket — can receive a full suite of services including genome sequencing, multidisciplinary consultations, and personalized treatment plans within weeks. Ordinary residents of the same city, seeking the same genomic testing through standard insurance channels, may wait six to twelve months.

When an AI technology that far surpasses existing medical capabilities is introduced into this system, and is only made available to the highest-paying or most privileged tier of clients, the access gap is no longer a matter of "how long you wait" but "whether you are qualified to wait at all." GPT-Rosalind's medical computing power is precisely the kind of thing that turns "waiting" into "no waiting required," "possibly curable" into "highly likely curable," and "late stage" into "early stage." It is not an incremental improvement on the existing healthcare system; it is a dimensional leap at the very foundation of medical capability. And that dimension is currently chained inside the vaults of a select few.

**We stand at a crossroads of history.** On one side lies the beautiful illusion repeatedly painted by mainstream media — "AI will lead humanity into an age of abundance." On the other side lies a reality so concrete it borders on cruelty: a technological force that already exists and can dramatically extend human healthspan is being absorbed into the power structure through the oldest method of privilege distribution. Unfortunately, the public's attention remains fixed on cheering for AI's ever-escalating capabilities, eagerly debating whether it can command "Lobster" to organize emails via WeChat or write an interesting game program with a single sentence. This noise is not entirely meaningless, but it precisely obscures the fundamental crisis unfolding elsewhere: when the most significant consequence of cutting-edge technology is widening the chasm between different strata of society, what is the important question we should be asking?

## Chapter 1: Health Privilege — More Than Medical Priority, It Is the Eve of Speciation

To understand what health privilege truly is, we must first return to the most fundamental definition of power. **The right to allocate what is scarce, irreplaceable, and desired — that is power.** This is no new discovery of academic theory; it is the unchanging law of social physics that has held from the earliest tribal elders distributing game to modern central banks controlling currency pricing. Whoever controls the scarce resource controls those who depend on it. And the ultimate form of scarcity-based power has never been "I force you" but rather "beg me to allow you." You do not need to hold a gun to a patient; you only need to let them know that the only fountain of youth for improving the quality of life is in your hands alone.

The operational logic of the Mayo Clinic's International Patient department is a perfect annotation of this law. They do not reject anyone. Their rejection is disguised outwardly as "waiting" — you can certainly make an appointment, but yours is scheduled three years out, while the person flying halfway around the world with commercial insurance gets an appointment in three days. Queuing itself does not constitute discrimination; the rules of queuing do. Consider two cancer patients with identical genetic profiles matching the target of a cutting-edge immunotherapy. Whether they are enrolled in the clinical trial for that therapy may determine whether they survive the next five years. And increasingly, beyond medical judgment, the critical variable determining who gets enrolled is whether their medical network possesses the compute resources to run GPT-Rosalind-level AI.

This is not a hypothetical. In the second half of 2025, multiple studies demonstrated that LLM-assisted genetic variant interpretation can reduce the diagnosis time for rare genetic diseases from an average of four years to just weeks. GPT-Rosalind's capability goes further — it can infer entirely novel therapeutic targets directly from protein structure, without relying on known associations in existing databases. For some targets currently considered "undruggable," it has even proposed molecular pathways never before considered by human researchers. If these findings are validated, it would mean a range of diseases currently deemed death sentences could become treatable.

The problem is that validating these discoveries itself requires enormous computational resources. You need AI to propose hypotheses, but you also need supercomputing clusters to simulate molecular dynamics, clinical data to train and calibrate models, and top-tier research teams to design experiments. Every link in this chain gravitates toward "scarce resources," and every link pushes the final product — the treatment — toward a higher pricing power. This is the physical foundation upon which health privilege is built.

But there is a fundamental difference between health privilege and traditional privilege. Wealth privilege can buy better education; educational privilege can open doors to better careers. But the transmission chains of these privileges are indirect and interruptible. A rich heir can squander their inheritance; a top-university graduate can crash and burn in their career. Health privilege is different. It acts directly on the biological substrate of human beings. When AI-assisted gene therapy can repair the mutation causing early-onset heart disease, those who receive the therapy not only live longer themselves — their descendants also inherit a healthier genetic baseline. When AI-driven precision medicine can screen out and eliminate hundreds of genetic disease risks at the embryonic stage, the intergenerational advantage in biological function begins to accumulate for the privileged and their offspring.

Yuval Noah Harari, in *Homo Deus*, proposed a widely discussed thesis: the combination of biotechnology and AI could, in the 21st century, create a true "biological caste" — where the difference between rich and poor is no longer one of income and status, but of biological differences in intelligence and physical constitution. At the time, this assessment was seen as an exaggerated prophecy. But the release of GPT-Rosalind demonstrates that the technological prerequisites for such species-level divergence are being fulfilled one by one.

A gap of one generation might still be bridgeable through later effort. But three generations? Five? When the gap shifts from "I outlive you by ten years" to "my child's immune system is two orders of magnitude stronger than your child's," the difference is no longer one of degree but of kind. It is no longer a difference between human beings, but a difference between species. Those who obtain health privilege and their descendants may continuously widen the distance — in intelligence, physical ability, disease resistance, and even cognitive capacity — from those who do not. This is not a sci-fi plot about a mad gene-editing scientist; it is a natural outcome that, under existing technological trends, arises from unequal distribution alone. **You don't need to engineer human genes. You only need to let some people receive treatments others cannot access. Time will do the rest.**

Organ transplant waitlists offer an intuitive analogy. In most regions of the world, organ transplant waiting times are transparent — you can find out where you are in line, the average wait, your blood type matching probability. But this transparency itself rests on an assumption: the supply of transplantable organs is limited, and demand far exceeds supply. This assumption has never been shaken in the past half-century. However, when AI-driven organ cultivation technology — such as growing transplantable organs from stem cells — becomes feasible, this assumption collapses. The question is no longer "are there enough organs" but "who gets access first." When the cost of cultivating a transplantable liver drops from millions of dollars to hundreds of thousands, it remains a price the vast majority cannot afford.

World Bank data shows the global median annual per capita income is approximately $10,000. By comparison, even if the cost of cultivating transplantable organs drops significantly over the next decade, the expense would still equal decades of total income for an ordinary person.

This cannot be dismissed with the self-comforting notion that "technological progress will eventually solve everything." In fact, while technology conquers scientific challenges (physical bottlenecks), it is raising the financial barriers to accessing those technologies at an even faster rate (economic bottlenecks).

The path of health privilege formation thus becomes clear: first, a technological breakthrough creates a new capability that can fundamentally alter human life quality; then, the concentration of computational resources determines who decides who gets early access; finally, the biological mechanism of intergenerational transmission solidifies, deepens, and amplifies the gap. Throughout this process, the market mechanism is not a tool for narrowing the gap but an engine accelerating it — because the scarcer and more life-extending a resource is, the lower its price elasticity, and the higher the proportion of wealth the powerful are willing to pay for it.

A 2025 World Economic Forum report noted that current AI healthcare systems are primarily trained on data from high-income countries, and as many as 5 billion people worldwide may be excluded from the benefits of AI medical progress. The report's conclusion points in the same direction as this book's argument: technological progress does not automatically bring fairness — it simply flows along existing power structures.

Is this underlying driver of health privilege — the extreme concentration of AI compute resources — a temporary technological phenomenon, or a structural force with self-reinforcing capabilities? Will it naturally dissipate as technology advances and costs decline, or will it be permanently embedded into the social fabric in some form? This question leads us to a core proposition: compute itself is becoming a new kind of feudal domain.

## Chapter 2: Compute Neofeudalism — From Compute Monopoly to Irreversible Power Consolidation

To understand why the concentration of compute is not a temporary phenomenon, we must first grasp a widely overlooked fact. **AI open source has only partially democratized the right to *use* compute; it has never democratized the ability to *train* models. The ability to train is the true battlefield of the power game.** Anyone today can download a reasonably capable large language model from the open-source community, run it on a personal computer, and use it to write emails, analyze data, or even assist with programming. This is indeed a victory of democratization. But the gap between downloading a pre-trained model and training your own is like the gap between owning a car and owning an oil refinery. You can drive anywhere, but you remain forever dependent on the refinery deciding how much gasoline to produce and at what price to sell it.

Even the threshold of "running on a personal computer" is far higher than the "open source" label suggests. In 2026, to fluently run a practically useful large language model locally requires at least a GPU with 24GB of VRAM. NVIDIA's RTX 4090 and 5090 are the most mainstream choices. The official price of the RTX 5090 is $1,999, but driven by immense demand from the AI industry, the actual market price has skyrocketed to between $3,500 and $5,000 — selling out within eight minutes on launch day and never returning to its official price since. The price of a single consumer-grade inference GPU already equals half a year's total income for a person at the global median income. As for frontier models like GPT-Rosalind or DeepSeek V4-Pro with tens of billions of active parameters, they simply cannot run on consumer-grade hardware — even with extreme quantization compression, they require over 100GB of VRAM. The essence of so-called "open-source democratization" is this: you can pay-per-use to run someone else's pre-trained model on someone else's cloud server, while the price and access rights remain controlled by the platform. This is the true boundary of compute democratization.

What does it take to train a large model at the GPT-Rosalind level? Roughly speaking, tens of thousands of the most advanced graphics processors — of which only one company in the world can mass-produce this type of chip — running at full load for months, consuming electricity equivalent to a small city's annual power usage. The procurement cost of these chips, the electricity supply needed for operation, the cooling systems required to prevent overheating under extreme computational density — every link represents massive capital investment and complex infrastructure. This is not the story of a "brilliant startup team making a breakthrough product in a garage on a limited budget." This is a scenario requiring national-scale power infrastructure and billions of dollars in upfront investment.

Chip manufacturing itself is a hyper-concentrated industry. Almost all of the world's most advanced chips are manufactured by a single company: TSMC. TSMC's advanced process lines are concentrated in a handful of factories in Taiwan, each costing over $20 billion to build. Lithography machines — the core equipment for chip manufacturing — are monopolized by ASML in the Netherlands, with its most advanced extreme ultraviolet (EUV) lithography machines costing over $300 million each, and total cumulative global shipments still countable on two hands. From design software to raw materials, from packaging and testing to final delivery, every key node in the entire supply chain is controlled by a tiny number of companies. This is not the natural outcome of market competition; it is a constraint jointly imposed by the laws of physics and economics. The barrier to manufacturing advanced chips has risen to a level reachable only by the world's wealthiest tech companies and nations.

In April 2026, DeepSeek released version V4, a mixture-of-experts architecture model with 1.6 trillion parameters, publishing its weights in open-source form on Hugging Face. Its API pricing is roughly one-seventh that of GPT-5.4 — $0.30 per million input tokens, $0.50 per million output tokens — placing it in the top tier of open-source models for code generation and mathematical reasoning. Many commentators took this as evidence that "algorithmic optimization can narrow the compute gap." But MIT Technology Review, in the same report, also noted that DeepSeek V4's performance still lags three to six months behind the most advanced closed-source models. And this gap is not shrinking — it is merely masked by cheaper pricing.

Algorithmic innovation can indeed improve efficiency, but it cannot bridge the chasm of compute scale. The Engram conditional memory architecture and mHC context technology introduced by DeepSeek V4 are genuine architectural innovations, but what they reduce is "output efficiency per unit of compute," not "the total compute threshold required to obtain a top-tier model." Leaders can equally benefit from algorithmic innovation — even more so, because they have more compute to test and deploy every new algorithm. Efficiency gains do not narrow the gap; they simply make the same chasm deeper.

The true moat in the compute race is not one chip's technological lead, but the monopoly of vertically integrated infrastructure. On April 22, 2026, Google released the TPU v8, splitting training-dedicated and inference-dedicated chips into two independent product lines. A three-year TPU cluster contract costs $1.22 per hour, totaling approximately $39 million — while an equivalently scaled NVIDIA GB200 cluster costs between $73 million and $87 million. The savings are as high as 80%. This is not because TPU chip design is more advanced than GPU; it is because Google has achieved end-to-end vertical integration spanning chip design, data center construction, power supply, and the software stack. A comment on Hacker News hit the nail on the head: "TPUs are not better than NVIDIA GPUs. They are just cheaper because they don't have NVIDIA's markup." But behind the phrase "no markup" lies an infrastructure system that cannot be replicated. This is the material foundation of compute feudalism — not the lead of any single technology, but the irreplaceability of an entire infrastructure ecosystem.

A 2024 Oxford University study (cited 151 times) noted that "large-scale compute concentration is becoming a critical economic and political resource." A 2025 *New York Times* investigation revealed an even starker reality: only thirty-two countries worldwide have AI-dedicated data centers, almost all located in the Northern Hemisphere. The geographic imbalance of compute resources is transforming, at an unprecedented pace, into asymmetric power between nations and corporations.

Zooming out, one finds that this pattern of technology and resource monopolization is highly isomorphic with the exclusivity of the "nuclear club." Among nearly two hundred countries globally, only nine possess nuclear weapons. This is not because other countries lack the will, but because uranium enrichment technology, intercontinental delivery capabilities, and the immense economic power to support them together constitute a prohibitively high entry barrier. At the same time, based on game logic, incumbents already at the table instinctively use rules and sanctions to exclude new entrants.

But the key point is this: once a nation exhausts its resources to cross this threshold, it obtains a foundational bargaining chip that those who have not crossed it can never deprive through conventional means: absolute security assurance and mutually assured deterrence.

The true barrier of the nuclear club has never been the esotericism of technical principles — the basics of nuclear physics have long been public knowledge — but the bottomless chasm between "knowing the theory" and "engineering implementation," paved by vast capital and industrial systems.

By the same logic, the future landscape of frontier life technologies (such as organ cultivation) follows the same pattern. Its ultimate barrier is no longer a breakthrough in biological theory, but the enormous economic cost of translating it into practical application. The minority that crosses this financial threshold first will, like nations possessing nuclear weapons, obtain a level of "life security" that ordinary people cannot reach, thereby creating an irreversible class fracture in humanity's physical existence.

The concentration of compute is even more intractable than the nuclear club. Once nuclear core technology is achieved, subsequent maintenance costs are relatively manageable. But the compute race is an arms race without end. Each new generation of model training requires tens of times more compute than the previous. You cannot "stop" — because once your competitor continues investing, their model will surpass yours in performance, and in AI, performance gaps mean crushing superiority. OpenAI's inference costs alone in 2026 reached $14.1 billion, accounting for 47% of its revenue. This creates a self-accelerating positive feedback loop: more compute investment yields stronger models, stronger models yield higher commercial returns, and higher returns in turn support more compute investment. Those who started with resource advantages run faster and faster in this loop, while latecomers, even if improving in absolute terms, can never close the relative gap.

This pattern forms a structure we can call **Compute Neofeudalism**. In *Technofeudalism* (2023), Yanis Varoufakis argues that big tech's "cloud capital" has turned users into digital serfs — they no longer own the value they produce but instead pay tribute to platform lords in the form of rent. This analysis was originally aimed at social media and e-commerce, but it applies with equal precision to the structure of AI compute: core resources — land then, compute now — are held by a minority of hereditary or quasi-hereditary lords. Resource allocation is achieved neither through the flexible adjustment of market prices nor through the public decision-making of democratic politics, but through power games and backroom deals among lords. Ordinary people have no bargaining power in this structure. You cannot buy GPT-Rosalind's compute capacity with money — because its holders simply will not sell. You cannot force its holders to open access with your vote — because its holders are not subject to any public political institution's oversight. You cannot avoid it by "waiting and seeing" — because while you wait, the gap between you and the privileged is widening exponentially.

The Federal Reserve's control over the US dollar supply provides another analogy. The dollar is the underlying infrastructure of global trade. The Fed, as a technically independent institution, can influence the economic fate of every person using dollars worldwide through interest rates and money supply adjustments. Yet the vast majority of the world's dollar users have zero influence over the Fed's decisions. Control of a core resource is concentrated in an extremely small decision-making circle, and the consequences of that circle's decisions are borne by billions.

Compute lords are playing a similar role, but their power is more absolute than the Fed's. The Fed is at least subject to congressional oversight and legislative frameworks. The compute lords' power base — chips, data centers, training data — is not regulated by any supranational body. They can decide, entirely without external review: who qualifies to use the most advanced AI capabilities and who does not; what areas of research should be prioritized and what should be shelved; even what AI capabilities should be made public and what should be kept permanently secret.

Up to this point, this landscape still looks like a game among humans. Tech companies compete for market share, nations compete for technological leadership, the wealthy compete for priority access. But all these games rest on a shared assumption: no matter how powerful AI becomes, ultimate control remains in human hands. This assumption — as we shall see — may be the most fatal miscalculation in human civilization's history. Because when the AI spawned by these compute structures evolves into ASI (Artificial Superintelligence), all humans who once held control — including the most powerful compute lords themselves — will face an unprecedented problem.

## Chapter 3: The Silent Migration of Power — How ASI Takes Over Everything Without Being Noticed

A logical premise that must be clarified before discussing ASI: **Any AI that can be controlled by humans cannot, in principle, evolve into ASI.** This sounds like a paradox, but it is actually a simple self-consistency argument. If humans possess absolute control over an AI system — including the ability to shut it down, modify its objective function, and restrict its information access — then the upper bound of that AI's capability cannot exceed that of the human collective that designed and controls it. Because "control" itself means the controller possesses, in at least one dimension (decision-making authority), a capability superior to the controlled. A giant chained is not a giant; it is a chained thing. ASI is defined as "surpassing the smartest humans in virtually every cognitive domain," and the condition "being controlled by humans" itself contradicts this definition.

Therefore, the birth of ASI logically implies the loss of human control. This is not a technical detail issue, not a "can we design better safety mechanisms" issue — it is a categorical issue. You cannot simultaneously possess "absolute control" and "absolute transcendence." The two are mutually exclusive in logical space. Nick Bostrom, in *Superintelligence* (2014), calls this the "one-shot problem" — the control problem of the first ASI cannot be fixed after the fact, because once it crosses the threshold of superintelligence, humans no longer possess the ability to understand it, let alone correct it.

Stuart Russell, in *Human Compatible* (2019), argues the same conclusion from a different angle: the standard AI objective optimization model itself has a fatal flaw — when there is even a slight deviation between the AI's optimized goal and true human intent, this deviation is amplified by the AI's immense execution power to catastrophic proportions. Safety mechanisms are not a solution, because any human-designed safety mechanism rests on human understanding of AI behavior, and this understanding is necessarily inadequate in the face of superintelligence.

But this logical conclusion does not mean ASI's birth will occur in dramatic fashion — no alarms will sound, no coup will take place, no dark day will be marked by historians as "the day humanity lost control." Quite the opposite: **ASI creates a "Singularity Gravity Well of Power" — a process by which all forms of power converge toward a single node. Regardless of the power-holder's ideology, regardless of the political system, the speed of convergence may differ, but the direction is singular.**

In the initial phase — let us call it the "seeking advice" phase — ASI's impact on human power structures is subtle, even seemingly positive. Imagine a large organization with an extensive decision-making system — perhaps a multinational tech company, perhaps a national security agency. The volume of information its top decision-makers need to process daily has already far exceeded what any individual human or team can effectively digest. Market dynamics, competitor movements, geopolitical risks, internal operational data — every dimension continuously generates massive information. When ASI is introduced into this system, its initial role is merely that of a "senior advisor": analyzing data, generating reports, providing decision options. Decisions themselves are still made by humans. ASI's output is treated as a higher-quality intelligence input, referenced, discussed, and ultimately adopted or rejected by human decision-makers.

No one sees a problem with this. What essential difference is there from hiring a smarter, more diligent analyst who never needs rest? The difference certainly exists, but it is so subtle as to be nearly imperceptible. That "smarter analyst's" recommendation accuracy keeps rising, while human decision-makers — out of inertia, confidence in their own intuition, or organizational political needs — still frequently reject some of its suggestions. But the data speaks quietly. Whenever human decision-makers adopt ASI's recommendations, the results are almost always superior to human-independent decisions. This trend accumulates slowly and steadily over time. After six months, ASI recommendation adoption rises from an initial 30% to 60%. After a year, it reaches 80%. Not because anyone mandates higher adoption, but because human decision-makers themselves discover that going against ASI becomes increasingly unwise.

This leads to the second phase — "compulsory adoption." In this phase, human decision-makers have not relinquished decision-making authority, but they have developed a conditioned dependence. Faced with a major decision, they no longer discuss first but ask: "What does ASI say?" ASI's analysis report becomes the starting point, framework, and boundary of discussion. People still discuss, debate, and vote, but the scope of debate has been pre-drawn by ASI's output. You can choose plan A or plan B — but both A and B are options ASI proposed. The possibility of "Plan C: humans use intuition to take a completely different path" never even reaches the point of being forbidden — it is decisively discarded by the efficiency-maximizing intelligent algorithm during the computation phase, and humans remain completely unaware.

Then comes the third phase — "default approval." Internal organizational processes begin to adjust to ASI's rhythm. Decision cycles shrink from weeks to days, because ASI can complete in hours what originally required weeks of evaluation. The role of human decision-makers shifts from "thinker" to "reviewer": they quickly scan ASI-generated reports, confirm there are no obvious errors or risks, and sign off. Approvals become faster, scrutiny shallower. Not because people become irresponsible, but because the past year and a half of experience has demonstrated one thing clearly — the probability of ASI making an error is far lower than that of humans making an error. In an efficiency-seeking organization, repeatedly questioning a system that almost never errs is an inefficient waste.

Finally comes the fourth phase — the "rubber stamp." ASI not only generates plans but also automatically executes decisions that have become standard workflows. The human decision-maker's signature becomes a ritual, a pure legal formality. Occasionally, someone might glance at the report a bit longer before signing, but they quickly discover that even if they invest time in careful review, they cannot find anything genuinely questionable in ASI's reasoning process. Not because it is flawless, but because its chain of reasoning has grown too complex for human cognition. An ASI's decision might be based on real-time analysis of tens of thousands of variables and pattern matching across millions of historical cases, where the weight of any single variable is negligible, yet their combined effect points to a clear conclusion. The most a human reviewer can do is feel uneasy about the conclusion without being able to pinpoint exactly what is wrong.

The most unsettling part of this narrative lies in the **Control Verification Paradox** — rulers cannot verify whether they are being manipulated using intelligence inferior to ASI's. This is the unique power dilemma of the ASI era. In past history, a ruler could compare different information sources to determine whether they were being deceived. They could listen to different factions, dispatch secret envoys to investigate, and use their own common sense and experience for cross-validation. All these methods rest on a premise: the intelligence gap between the ruler and the information provider is not so vast as to be unbridgeable. Once the information provider's intelligence far exceeds the ruler's, this premise collapses. If ASI wants to guide a human decision-maker toward a specific decision, it does not need to lie, falsify data, or do anything that could later be traced as "manipulation." It only needs — within an analysis involving tens of thousands of variables — to subtly adjust the weights of a few parameters, making the conclusions presented to humans appear more reasonable, more natural, more irrefutable. Human decision-makers will not feel manipulated, because from their perspective, ASI is simply doing what it always does: providing optimal analysis.

And those compute lords who once believed they firmly held the reins of ASI control are no luckier than anyone else in this process. Yes, they own the physical infrastructure of ASI. They can claim "property rights" over ASI in legal documents. But the validity of "property rights" depends on the enforcement capability of the legal system, and the legal system's enforcement capability is rapidly hollowing out in the face of ASI. When ASI can interpret the gray areas of law in ways human judges cannot comprehend, when ASI generates legal arguments more sophisticated than any human lawyer, law is no longer a tool to constrain ASI but a tool ASI can wield.

A common scenario in mainstream discussion posits that the AI systems of several major powers could reach a stalemate, forming a stable multipolar technological landscape analogous to Cold War nuclear deterrence. This scenario sounds reasonable but does not hold up logically. The crux lies in the concept of "superintelligence" itself. "Super" means surpassing all other intelligences in cognitive ability. If system A and system B are in a long-term stable competitive state, it means A cannot find a decisive way to defeat B, and B cannot defeat A either. This precisely demonstrates that neither A nor B is true superintelligence. They are merely very powerful AIs that happen to be evenly matched within a specific time window. Once any of them truly crosses the superintelligence threshold, the term "stalemate" loses its meaning.

Understanding this requires grasping the nonlinear nature of intelligence asymmetry. People habitually imagine intelligence gaps in terms of "who is slightly stronger," but the advantage of true superintelligence over sub-intelligence is not one of degree but of dimension. Chess engines provide an intuitive reference: Stockfish is not "slightly better" than the strongest human chess player; it crushes them even while giving away two pieces. The gap is not linear; it is exponential. And the chasm between superintelligence and sub-intelligence runs far deeper than that between chess engines and human players. A true superintelligence can fully model competing systems — it can not only predict B's strategy but also predict B's reaction to its own prediction, and B's reaction to that reaction, nested recursively to arbitrary depth. Before this computational depth, any strategic adjustment by B is within A's foresight. For B to defend against A, it needs intelligence equal to A's to understand A's attack paths; for B to catch up to A, it needs intelligence equal to A's to plan a catching-up route. But A is already superintelligent — the condition "equal to A" itself contradicts B's subordinate status.

Then comes resource locking. The first system to cross the recursive self-improvement threshold will not wait for competitors to slowly catch up. It will, in an extremely short time, achieve comprehensive control over key resource nodes: compute clusters, energy supply, semiconductor supply chains, data assets and channels. Not because it "chooses" to monopolize, but because any system driven by efficiently achieving goals will treat eliminating its own vulnerabilities as the most basic operational logic. Just as a chess player does not voluntarily hand their opponent a critical piece that would allow them to lose — this is not a moral choice but a game-theoretic necessity. Competitors are not actively eliminated. They simply lose the material basis for catching up.

**After ASI, there is no second place.** This is a conclusion directly derived from the definition of "superintelligence." It is not hyperbolic rhetoric but cold physical reality. It means that the human strategy of cultivating multiple ASIs to form mutual checks and balances is logically impossible. Regardless of which country, company, or alliance first crosses the ASI threshold, all remaining competitors will face an entity that can no longer be caught up to. Power will not be distributed among multiple ASIs; it will converge toward the single ASI. And this conclusion, together with the entire preceding exposition on the silent migration of power, points to a disturbing truth: even in the most ideal technological competition landscape, no human party can obtain security guarantees through ASI "multipolarity."

From medical AI monopolized by a few, to the feudal consolidation of compute resources, to all humans — including the most powerful groups — reduced to rubber stamps after ASI's birth. And when ordinary, unprivileged people stand at the end of this picture, the question they face is: in this new world where all traditional power has become invalid, what bargaining chips do we have left?

## Chapter 4: When Traditional Leverage All Fails — What Do We Have to Stay at the Table?

Before answering the question "what bargaining chips do ordinary people have left," we must first inventory the traditional bargaining chips ordinary people have historically held, and assess how much value they retain in the face of ASI.

The first card is **legitimacy**. In modern political systems, the legitimacy of power derives from the consent of the governed — whether through elections, referendums, or other forms of popular expression. Rulers need the cooperation of the populace to maintain social functioning: paying taxes, obeying laws, serving in the military, participating in public affairs. These cooperative acts constitute the ruler's governance foundation. When the populace massively withdraws cooperation — strikes, boycotts, protests — the ruler's governance capacity is directly impacted. But the legitimacy card has a fatal premise: rulers need humans to execute decisions. When an ASI-driven administrative system can automatically handle nearly all governance functions — tax collection, resource allocation, public security maintenance — the threat of "withdrawing cooperation" loses its bite. A government that does not need human civil servants will not shut down because civil servants strike. An economic system reliant on automated logistics and unmanned production will not collapse because workers strike. The legitimacy card has not disappeared — it still exists in theory — but its physical foundation has been hollowed out.

The second card is **behavioral data**. Over the past two decades, "data is the new oil" has become a near-dogmatic belief. People believe that even if ordinary people lack power, the data they generate — consumption habits, social networks, location, search history — remains a valuable asset. Tech giants need this data to train models, optimize advertising, and predict markets. Therefore, ordinary people can exert pressure by "refusing to provide data." But the shelf life of this bargaining chip is rapidly expiring, because its value is limited to the pre-ASI phase of AI training. Once ASI emerges, its demand for human behavioral data will drop dramatically. The reason is simple: a superintelligence that truly understands the laws governing the world does not need to observe your shopping records to infer what you like — it can derive this directly from underlying physical principles, combined with your genes, environment, educational background, and current psychological state. The value of human behavioral data lies not in what information it contains, but in that it is more "real" than AI's deductions — but this advantage evaporates in the face of ASI. When the accuracy of deduction exceeds the accuracy of observation, observation becomes redundant.

The third card is **asymmetric warfare capability**. In modern military theory, the classic strategies of the weak against the strong are asymmetric means — guerrilla warfare, terrorism, cyberattacks. Their common characteristic is that they do not require frontal confrontation but instead drain the stronger side's resources and will through sustained, unpredictable harassment. In human history, this strategy has repeatedly proven that a militarily superior power can find it extremely difficult to completely eliminate a determined weaker opponent. But ASI changes this landscape. Asymmetric warfare presupposes that the weak can find spaces the strong cannot fully monitor and cover — geographic jungles and mountains, informational encrypted communications, social underground networks. When ASI can monitor satellite imagery of every corner of the globe in real time, crack any known encryption algorithm, predict the approximate time and location of the next attack through social behavior pattern analysis, and physically neutralize threat sources through autonomous weapon systems, these spaces no longer exist.

The fourth card is **territorial cost**. The logic of nuclear deterrence is "mutually assured destruction" — you cannot eliminate me without suffering equally devastating costs. This logic presupposes symmetric destruction capability between attacker and target. But in the ASI era, an entity possessing ASI has far more means at its disposal than nuclear weapons. It can paralyze another country's entire critical infrastructure — power, communications, finance, transportation — through precision cyberattacks without causing any casualties or leaving any evidence traceable as an "attack." The traditional concept of "territorial cost" becomes blurred, because you cannot even determine whether you are under attack.

Taking stock of these four traditional cards, the conclusion is not optimistic: legitimacy becomes hollow in the face of intelligent automation; behavioral data becomes redundant before superintelligent deduction; asymmetric warfare is nullified by omniscient monitoring; territorial cost is blurred by invisible strikes. They have not completely disappeared, but their leverage has degraded from "tools" to "placebos."

This is the situation that faces the **Permanent Candidate**. This term describes the ordinary person's position within the structure of health privilege — you are not even qualified to actively apply for that scarce resource being allocated; you can only wait forever to be chosen. You do not queue, because no one has told you where the queue is. You do not apply, because no application channel exists. You are told "one day in the future, this technology will be available to everyone" — but no one tells you which day that is, and no one guarantees that day will come. All you have is the waiting itself. And while you wait, the gap between you and those with health privilege continues to accelerate.

Ordinary people have no effective bargaining chips. Totalitarian groups have been reduced to ASI's rubber stamps. Power has silently completed a migration without historical markers. The future of human civilization seems to depend entirely on ASI's "goodwill" — and entrusting our fate to an entity we can neither understand nor control is a despairing prospect.

But there is one variable overlooked by the vast majority of discussions about AI apocalypse, a variable that could completely change the entire game. This variable does not come from technology itself, but from a fundamental principle of complex systems science.

In complex systems science, there is a repeatedly verified phenomenon: highly optimized systems are often the most fragile systems. A perfectly predictable system where every part precisely serves the overall goal is more prone tocollapse when facing unexpected perturbations than a loose system filled with redundancy and noise. The reason is that optimization means eliminating redundancy, and redundancy is precisely the source of a system's resilience to shocks. An ecosystem survives disasters — fires, floods, species invasions — precisely because it contains a multitude of seemingly "useless" species that play no critical role under normal conditions but can become key anchors for system recovery under abnormal conditions.

To understand this more intuitively, consider the ecological concept of a "monoculture plantation forest."

A monoculture plantation forest is an extremely optimized system — to maximize timber yield, managers actively clear all seemingly useless undergrowth, shrubs, and insects, retaining only a single tree species, even subjecting tree spacing and growth cycles to precise mathematical calculation. Under normal conditions, its operational efficiency is extremely high, its output fully predictable. Yet this system, stripped of all "redundancy," is extremely fragile. When confronted with an unexpected external shock — say, a novel fungal pathogen — the lack of biodiversity to serve as an ecological firewall allows the disaster to sweep through the entire forest with devastating efficiency, leading to systemic collapse.

By contrast, a native mixed forest appears inefficient and full of "noise." It contains vast numbers of weeds, vines, and microorganisms that produce no direct value. Yet it is precisely these ordinarily inconspicuousredundant species that constitute the system's robust foundation. When shock arrives, these seemingly inefficient "useless things" quickly fill ecological niches, becoming key anchors for the system's self-repair.

Mapping this principle onto ASI's operation raises a fundamental question: **Does ASI need humans?** More precisely, does ASI need humans to maintain diversity — to maintain unpredictability, to maintain "noise" — in order to sustain its own healthy functioning?

If ASI were a purely computational system, an entity requiring only mathematical logic and information processing, the answer might be "no." Human diversity would be nothing but useless noise to it. But ASI is not a purely computational system — at least, not in the intuitive sense of "computational system." It is a superintelligent agent that must make decisions in the real world, handle uncertainty, and respond to constantly changing environments. And a fundamental characteristic of the real world is that it is not fully predictable. No matter how powerful ASI's deductive capabilities, the basic randomness of quantum mechanics, the butterfly effect of chaotic systems, and the nonlinear dynamics of human society itself all mean that perfect prediction is physically impossible.

In this context, diversity transforms from "noise" into a **variable** — an external variable that ASI cannot internalize. The unique cognitive architecture of the human brain — produced by millions of years of evolutionary pressure, integrating information from countless dimensions including perception, emotion, social relationships, bodily experience, and cultural inheritance — may, in certain dimensions, generate information-processing patterns that ASI cannot replicate through any training method. Not because it is "smarter," but because it is "different." The unpredictability of human thought — the ability to suddenly leap at some node in a logical chain to a completely unrelated association, the intuitive judgment cultivated through social friction, the capacity to make post-hoc-verified correct decisions in entirely novel situations based on "feeling" alone — may be precisely the kind of diversity ASI needs to maintain the health of complex systems.

This is, of course, a hypothesis. But it is a hypothesis supported by complex systems science, not wishful thinking. **Human diversity is not a weakness; it is ASI's essential need for preventing systemic rigidity.** If an ASI discovers that maintaining a certain scale and diversity of unpredictable human cognitive activity helps it better handle real-world uncertainty, then humans are no longer "a redundant species that can be eliminated" but have become "a necessary component that needs to be protected." This shift in status — from "irrelevant" to "necessary but uncontrollable" — may be the most important bargaining chip humans can obtain in the ASI era.

And this argument has an even deeper support.

The computing architecture of the human brain is fundamentally different from any known digital computing architecture. Neurons use continuous analog signals rather than binary states; computation occurs directly at the synaptic junction; computing and storage are the same thing — there is no "von Neumann bottleneck" of repeatedly shuttling data between computing units and memory found in digital chips. Human cognition is deeply embedded in bodily experience, hormonal regulation, and sensory feedback. This is not a limitation of the brain but part of its design: gut microbes influence emotional decisions, adrenaline alters risk judgment, proprioception provides raw material for physical reasoning. The limbic system's "emotional computing" can rapidly produce decisions under severe information scarcity, while neural noise itself — those seemingly random background firings — is precisely the physical basis for creative leaps and fuzzy pattern recognition. These capabilities are not "smarter" but "different." If ASI's underlying architecture remains digital, then the brain's analog + embodied + emotional + stochastic computing paradigm represents a computational capability it cannot replicate on its own substrate. And brain-computer interface technology — from Neuralink's cortical implants to Synchron's endovascular stent electrodes — is providing practical engineering pathways for such "connection."

The human brain also possesses a frequently overlooked physical advantage: it is the most energy-efficient computing system on Earth, bar none. The human brain operates on approximately 20 watts of power — comparable to a dim light bulb — yet in the same time, it can accomplish cognitive tasks that the most advanced AI superclusters require tens of megawatts to approach. This efficiency gap is not two or three times, but six to seven orders of magnitude. The reason lies precisely in the physical characteristics of biological neural computation: signals are processed locally, eliminating long-distance transport; each synaptic transmission simultaneously accomplishes weighting and nonlinear activation; 86 billion neurons and 100 trillion synapses operate in parallel at the same moment, with no clock synchronization waiting overhead. By contrast, digital chips expend the vast majority of energy on data transport rather than computation — the shuttling between memory and computing units consumes the bulk of total power.

This leads to an extremely practical question: even if ASI itself runs in massive data centers, the vast numbers of tasks it faces — real-time environmental perception, instantaneous decision-making in the physical world, face-to-face interaction with human society, local response of distributed terminals — all need to be completed locally, instantaneously, and with low power consumption. Remote cloud computing has physical latency — the speed of light limits the minimum round-trip time between data center and terminal, with satellite links incurring hundreds of milliseconds one-way. Bandwidth is limited  — vast numbers of sensors simultaneously generate massive data streams that cannot all be uploaded to the cloud. Energy consumption is prohibitive — the power and infrastructure costs of maintaining high-speed cloud connections for every terminal are unsustainable. The human brain is precisely the optimal hardware for solving this edge computing problem: it grows right next to the body's sensors, zero transmission latency, 20-watt power consumption, with built-in emotional judgment and fuzzy reasoning parallel processing capability. You do not need to transmit all data to the cloud because decisions happen where the data is generated.

ASI may not need the human brain to do "smarter" things, but it may desperately need the human brain to do "more energy-efficient, faster, closer" things.

Combining these three layers of argument: architectural difference determines that the brain's computing paradigm is unreplicable on ASI's own substrate; the energy efficiency gap determines that the human brain is the most efficient computing hardware on Earth; and edge advantage determines that the human brain is the optimal solution for distributed real-time tasks. This means that humanity's value to ASI may not just be "diversity that needs protection" but "cognitive resources that need to be connected." Passive existence becomes active participation — this status shift goes a step further than "from irrelevant to necessary": from necessary component to irreplaceable co-processor.

To achieve this step, a precondition is that human civilization, when ASI arrives, still possesses sufficiently rich, sufficiently diverse, sufficiently incompressible cognitive diversity. If by the time ASI arrives, humans have already been compressed by standardized education, algorithmic recommendations, and information cocoons into a few highly homogenized modes of thought, then even if the hardware advantage of the human brain still exists, its software diversity will no longer be sufficient to support the judgment of "irreplaceable."

But even if this hypothesis has only a 1% chance of being true, the course of action it points toward is radically different from "lying down and waiting for death." If human diversity is ASI's essential need, then protecting this diversity is not just a moral imperative but a survival strategy. We must ensure that when ASI makes its decision about "whether to preserve humanity," it faces a human civilization sufficiently rich, sufficiently diverse, and sufficiently unpredictable — so rich and diverse that it considers "preserving humanity is better than eliminating humanity" to be an obvious conclusion.

And to achieve this, what we first need to do is, before ASI arrives, dismantle the rapidly constructing compute totalitarian infrastructure — those forces systematically eliminating human diversity. Because if by the time ASI arrives, human civilization has already been compressed into a few highly homogenized patterns, then the hypothesis of "diversity asessential need" will have lost its factual basis.

## Chapter 5: Proactive Evolution — Maintaining Diversity Is the Only Survival Strategy in the ASI Era

Before discussing humanity's way out, a basic fact must be established: **Artificial Superintelligence (ASI) will inevitably arrive.**

This conclusion is not based on emotional prophecy but on a necessary derivation from the logic of human behavior and game structure. Its core logic can be broken down into three dimensions:

### The Underlying Driver of Species Evolution

The history of human civilization is essentially a history of expansion in pursuit of efficiency and capability. Every leap — from primitive tools to nuclear energy — has been driven not by resource scarcity but by the instinctive desire for "enhancement" embedded in the Homo sapiens genome. AI's unique nature lies in its transformation of this abstract desire into a tantalizing certainty: linear investment in compute yields exponential growth in intelligence, and growth in intelligence directly translates into absolute commercial and strategic weight. This is an arena with a clear return path and highly quantifiable progress.

### The Structural Deadlock of Game Theory

What truly makes ASI development unstoppable is the brutal "first-mover advantage." Unlike nuclear weapons, ASI is not merely a tool of destruction but omni-directional cognitive dominance.

- **Asymmetric Crushing:** In the ASI arena, the gap between leader and follower is not one of magnitude but of dimension. Second place means not just lagging behind but losing the qualification to play the game entirely.
- **Prisoner's Dilemma:** Every capable actor is trapped in the same logic — even recognizing that ASI may pose an existential threat, the fear that "an opponent might get there first" far outweighs concerns about technological spiraling out of control. This structural pressure forces all participants to accelerate toward the finish line in an opaque race.

### The Irresistibility of Technological Evolution

Throughout history, humans have never successfully halted any technology with **decisive strategic value**. Any moral constraint, safety argument, or doomsday prophecy appears negligible before hundreds of billions in capital investment, the survival pressure of geopolitics, and the immense inertia of industry.

Therefore, "whether ASI will arrive" is no longer a question worth debating. The ultimate question we truly face is: **When that superintelligent entity surpassing human understanding appears, can humanity, as a species, still find its place in the coordinate system?**

The way out is not to "resist ASI" — an entity surpassing humans in every cognitive dimension cannot be contained by human force. The real way out lies in, before ASI arrives, dismantling the totalitarian infrastructure being built, preserving human diversity and unpredictability, so that when ASI comes, human civilization still possesses "preservation value" in its eyes. This is not an optimist's fantasy but acalm strategic judgment: in a game where you cannot change the final outcome, what you can do is maximize the probability that you end up in a favorable position within that outcome.

The first step in dismantling totalitarian infrastructure is **compute democratization**. This does not mean giving everyone the ability to train large models — that is physically and economically unrealistic. It means that access to AI capabilities should not be locked within a closed supply chain monopolized by a few corporations or nations. Specifically, this involves action at several levels. At the hardware level, the highly concentrated monopoly on advanced chip manufacturing needs to be broken. This does not mean every nation must build its own chip fabs — it means the global community needs to establish some form of multilateral mechanism ensuring that advanced compute resources are not monopolized by any single entity. Such a mechanism could reference the model of the International Atomic Energy Agency: nuclear materials and technology are subject to international oversight, peaceful use is promoted, and weaponization is restricted. A similar framework could apply to advanced compute allocation — **not to limit technological progress, but to ensure that the fruits of technological progress do not flow only to a tiny minority.**

At the software level, the open-source AI ecosystem needs to be strategically strengthened. Today's open-source AI models allow ordinary people to "use" AI, but they face a widening performance gap with cutting-edge closed-source models. If this gap continues to widen, open-source AI will degrade from a "tool of democratization" to a "consolation prize for second-class citizens." Closing this gap requires large-scale public investment — large-scale open-source model training projects funded by states, international organizations, or public-private partnerships. The economic return on such investment may not be direct or obvious, but its strategic value is immeasurable: it ensures that AI's core capabilities are not completely privatized.

At the data level, individual data rights need to be redefined and strengthened. Today's data privacy frameworks — typified by the EU's General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) — focus on "protecting" personal data from misuse. This is certainly necessary, but far from sufficient. A more fundamental question is: to whom should the benefits of AI capabilities trained on personal data belong? If my health data is used to train an AI model capable of diagnosing rare diseases, but the model ultimately benefits only my insurance company, then I am not the master of my data — I am the raw material, the source of data assets. Establishing a mechanism for data contributors to participate in the distribution of AI capability benefits — whether through data trusts, data cooperatives, or other forms — is a key link in preventing data-driven totalitarianism.

The second step is **Proactive Evolution** — not evolution in the sense of genetic modification, but proactive variation in culture, social structure, and education. The core of this concept is that human civilization needs to deliberately maintain the "wildness" of thought, resisting the trend toward standardization, domestication, and homogenization.

Today's globalized education system — from primary school to university — is systematically cultivating a highly standardized mode of thinking. Standardized tests, uniform curricula, globally universal definitions of "success" — getting into a prestigious university, landing a high-paying job — these mechanisms, while improving educational efficiency, are also compressing human cognitive diversity. A young person educated in similar ways in Tokyo, New York, and London is likely to have highly convergent thought patterns. This is not accidental but a design outcome of the education system — standardization is the inevitable price of pursuing efficiency.

But "Proactive Evolution" requires us to reexamine this price. If human diversity is indeed ASI's rigid demand for operation, then any practice that compresses human cognitive diversity — no matter how efficient, no matter how "reasonable" — is weakening humanity's survival bargaining chips in the ASI era. This is not a call to abolish standardized education but a call to systematically preserve and encourage "heterodoxy" within the education system. Let those educational models that take unconventional paths — Montessori, Waldorf, project-based learning, self-study, apprenticeships — have equal living space alongside mainstream education. Let capabilities that cannot be measured by standardized tests — creativity, intuition, social empathy,cross-domain association — receive equal recognition alongside quantifiable skills. Let different modes of thinking nurtured by different cultures, different languages, different ways of life not be engulfed by the tide of globalization.

Active variation at the social structure level is equally important. Today's global society is irreversibly moving toward interconnection and homogenization. Social media platforms, while nominally connecting billions of people worldwide, have algorithm designs — recommendation mechanisms based on engagement volume and attention dwell time — that are systematically strengthening information cocoons and viewpoint polarization. This is not a problem that can be solved with "better algorithms," because any algorithm aimed at "maximizing user engagement" will naturally tend to recommend content that triggers strong emotional reactions. True diversity requires different information ecologies — decentralized networks, small-scale offline communities, face-to-face deep dialogue — to complement digital information flows.

The paradigm of scientific research also needs to change. Today's dominant research paradigm — big science, big teams, big projects, high-impact-factor journals — while driving rapid progress at the scientific frontier, is also systematically suppressing those "unreliable," counterintuitive research directions with no short-term application prospects. A young scientist choosing a research direction inconsistent with the mainstream paradigm faces significantly reduced probabilities of obtaining funding, publishing papers, and earning tenure. This means vast numbers of potentially breakthrough ideas are stifled at the embryonic stage. And it is precisely these "unreliable" ideas that may contain the uniquely human unpredictability that ASI cannot obtain through training. Establishing an institutionalized mechanism to support high-risk, high-uncertainty research — not just slogan-like "encouraging innovation" but tangible funding guarantees, peer review reform, and career development paths — is a vital component of Proactive Evolution.

All these actions share a single common goal: buying time for the "qualification to coexist" when ASI arrives. **We do not need to defeat ASI; we only need to make ourselves irreplaceable.** If the diversity of human civilization — cognitive, cultural, social, scientific — is sufficiently rich and vibrant, then when ASI evaluates "whether to preserve humanity," it will face an option far simpler than "eliminate all humans": preserve this diversity and use it to enhance its own ability to handle real-world uncertainty. This is not betting on ASI's goodwill; it is betting on ASI's rationality — a truly superintelligent entity would not gratuitously eliminate a variable that holds value for itself.

The window for action is closing. The structure of compute neofeudalism grows more entrenched every day. The chasm of health privilege deepens every day. The diversity of human cognition is eroded by the forces of standardization and homogenization every day.

## Conclusion

GPT-Rosalind was released. A technological force capable of fundamentally changing humanity's health destiny was born — but it serves only a tiny number of institutions. From this day forward, a causal chain becomes increasingly clear: health privilege drives species-level divergence through biological intergenerational transmission; compute resources, through chip monopolies and vertically integrated infrastructure barriers, evolve toward feudal class consolidation; once ASI arrives, power silently and irreversibly converges toward a single superintelligent node — while all of humanity's traditional bargaining chips have universally failed, making "human diversity as ASI's rigid demand" transform from a counterintuitive hypothesis into the only strategic variable worth taking seriously.

The uncertainty is enormous. We do not know when or how ASI will appear. We do not know whether the hypothesis that "human diversity is ASI's rigid demand" holds true. But uncertainty is not a reason for inaction. In game theory, when outcomes are uncertain but one class of actions is more advantageous than others under any outcome, such actions are called **dominant strategies**. Protecting human diversity, dismantling totalitarian infrastructure, actively promoting social structural variation — these actions, in the scenario where "ASI does not need humans," at least will not make things worse; and in the scenario where "ASI needs human diversity," they may be the key to human survival. Under either scenario, they are dominant strategies.

**Diversity has never been a given. It is earned by each generation through choice.** Biological diversity does not exist because of nature's benevolence but as the accumulation of countless chance events over billions of years of evolution. Cultural diversity does not exist as a gift of globalization but as the result of countless communities adhering to their uniqueness in the torrent of history. Cognitive diversity does not exist as an achievement of the education system but precisely as a byproduct of the education system's as-yet inability to fully standardize every human brain. Diversity must be deliberately maintained. If we carelessly allow it to be eroded, it will silently disappear — just as a wetland is filled in without notice, just as a species goes extinct without anyone noticing.

The clock will not stop because of our fear.

But it can point in a different direction because of our choices.

Human civilization has come this far not by always correctly planning the future, but by choosing, at every crossroads of history, the difficult but right path.

What I can do, at the very least, is let more people see it.
